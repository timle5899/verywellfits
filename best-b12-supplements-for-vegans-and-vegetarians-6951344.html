B12 is a crucial vitamin for blood cells, the nervous system, and DNA synthesis

Vitamin B12 is a nutrient that is important for your blood cells, nervous system, and DNA synthesis. It is naturally found primarily in animal foods such as fish, meat, eggs, poultry, and dairy. Therefore, if you follow a vegan or vegetarian diet, you are at greater risk of deficiency, and we recommend considering a B12 supplement as no vegan foods are naturally a significant source of B12. The upside is that "many vegan foods are fortified with B-12, including nutritional yeast and some plant milks, but supplementation makes it easier,” says Anna Titcomb, MS, RD, CSSD. 

Some common signs of B12 deficiency to look for include fatigue, heart palpitations, pale skin, or tingling in the hands and feet. While it can take years for a deficiency to form, if you are starting a vegan diet, it can help to begin supplementing early. There are various types of B12 you might see in supplements or multivitamins, though you'll most commonly see B12 in the form of cyanocobalamin.

When looking for a B12 supplement, consider whether you want a supplement with only B12 or a multivitamin that includes other nutrients important for vegans and vegetarians, such as vitamin D, omega-3 fats, iron, calcium, iodine, zinc, and selenium. You will also want to consider a dose appropriate for your needs and whether you prefer supplements in pill, spray, or sublingual (under the tongue) form.

Are B12 Supplements Beneficial for Vegans and Vegetarians?

B12 supplements are often important for vegans and vegetarians since vitamin B12 exists naturally only in animal foods in reliable amounts. While some foods such as fortified cereals and fortified nutritional yeast contain B12, you may have a challenging time getting enough if you are relying only on those foods.

“All vegans should have a reliable source of B12 whether that comes from a supplement or fortified foods like non-dairy milk, tofu, and nutritional yeast. Fortified foods and supplements are the only reliable source of vitamin B12 in a vegan diet,” says Geiger. 

While most vegans and many vegetarians will benefit from B12 supplements, the following groups, whether or not they are vegetarian or vegan, may benefit from B12 supplements:

  • People who are pregnant: The amount of vitamin B12 needed by pregnant people is 2.6 mcg per day, and 2.8 mcg while breastfeeding. This is higher than the amounts needed typically. If you are vegan or vegetarian, your baby may not receive enough B12, which can result in neural tube defects, developmental delays, and anemia.
  • Older adults: Many older adults don’t produce enough hydrochloric acid, which is needed to absorb vitamin B12 in food. Vitamin B12 deficiencies are therefore common in older adults, who may benefit from supplements.
  • People with gastrointestinal disorders: Individuals with certain disorders such as atrophic gastritis, celiac disease, Crohn’s disease, or those who have had intestinal surgery may struggle to absorb enough vitamin B12.
  • People who have had intestinal surgery: If you have had stomach or intestinal surgery, you may not make enough hydrochloric acid and intrinsic factor, which are needed for B12 absorption.
  • People taking certain medications: There are particular medications that can increase your risk of vitamin B12 deficiency. This includes the drug metformin, taken for prediabetes and diabetes, as well as proton pump inhibitors.

Who May Not Benefit From a Vegan B12 Supplement

If you follow a strictly vegan diet, you will need to supplement with vitamin B12 or to make sure you’re consuming enough from fortified foods. Vitamin B12 typically does not cause any harm, even at high doses, since the body does not store excess amounts. There are, however, certain situations in which you may want to exercise caution before using a B12 supplement:

  • Those getting enough vitamin B12 from food: If you follow a vegetarian diet and consume foods such as eggs and dairy, you may want to check with a healthcare provider to determine your B12 status. If you are getting enough vitamin B12 from foods, you may not require a B12 supplement. 
  • People taking certain medications: Vitamin B12 supplements can interact with certain medications. This includes gastric acid inhibitors such as omeprazole, lansoprazole cimetidine, or ranitidine. The drug metformin may also reduce vitamin B12 absorption. If you are using one of these medications, speak with a healthcare provider.

What to Look for in B12 Supplements for Vegans and Vegetarians

Independent Verification

Supplements that are independently verified are sent to a lab where they are tested to ensure they contain what they say they contain and are not contaminated with specific high-risk, common contaminants. However, it’s important to note:

  • Independent verification does not test to see if a product is effective or safe for everyone, and it does not ensure the supplement will not interact with other supplements or medications.
  • Not all independent verification is created equal. It is not uncommon for supplement companies to pay labs for certificates after conducting minimal to no testing. 
  • The independent verifications we can trust are: ConsumerLab.com, NSF, and USP. However, these certifications are difficult to obtain and/or expensive for manufacturers, so many companies choose not to get their products tested by one of these three organizations. 
  • Sometimes products tested by these three companies are more expensive to try to offset the cost they pay for certification.
  • Just because a supplement is not tested by one of these three companies, it does not mean it’s a bad product. We recommend doing some research on the reputability of the manufacturer, and calling up the manufacturer and their testing lab to determine their protocols and decide if you feel comfortable consuming the supplement.

Form

Vitamin B12 is sold in various forms including as capsules, sublingual tablets or lozenges (tablets that dissolve under your tongue), liquid drops, chewables, and gummies. 

The chemical form of B12 is either methylcobalamin, adenosylcobalamin, hydroxocobalamin, or cyanocobalamin. Methylcobalamin and adenosylcobalamin are the active forms of B12, while hydroxocobalamin and cyanocobalamin become active once they are converted to methylcobalamin or adenosylcobalamin. 

The most common form of B12 in supplements is cyanocobalamin. Titcomb says, “Cyanocobalamin is a great option as it's a more stable compound and typically cheaper as well.” 

Research does not demonstrate that there is a difference in absorption for different supplement forms of B12, so you can choose whichever form you prefer.

Ingredients & Potential Interactions

It is essential to carefully read the ingredient list and nutrition facts panel of a supplement to know which ingredients and how much of each ingredient is included, relative to the recommended daily value of that ingredient. Please bring the supplement label to a healthcare provider to review the different ingredients contained in the supplement and any potential interactions between these ingredients and other supplements and medications you are taking.

B12 supplements can interact or interfere with certain medications. This includes gastric acid inhibitors taken to treat digestive issues such as gastroesophageal reflux disease or peptic ulcer disease. These drugs, including omeprazole, lansoprazole, cimetidine, and ranitidine, can slow the release of hydrochloric acid into the stomach, which can interfere with B12 absorption. Metformin, a drug used to treat prediabetes and diabetes, can also reduce absorption of vitamin B12.

When choosing a vegan B12 supplement, be sure to read labels carefully. Geiger says to check the ingredients, as products such as capsules may contain gelatin, which is not suitable for vegans. If you’re using a multivitamin and want to ensure it is vegan, you should also check that the vitamin D and omega-3 come from plant sources. 

Dosage

According to the National Institutes of Health, the Recommended Dietary Allowances (RDA) for Vitamin B12 are as follows:

  • Birth to 6 months: Males and females: 0.4 mcg
  • 7-12 months: Males and females: 0.5 mcg
  • 1-3 years: Males and females: 0.9 mcg
  • 4-8 years: Males and females: 1.2 mcg
  • 9-13 years: Males and females: 1.8 mcg
  • 14-18 years: Males and females: 2.4 mcg, Pregnancy: 2.6 mcg, Lactating: 2.8 mcg
  • 19+ years: Males and females: 2.4 mcg, Pregnancy: 2.6 mcg, Lactating: 2.8 mcg

Vitamin B12 in supplements is typically sold in higher doses than the RDA because our bodies only absorb small amounts from supplements.

How Much is Too Much?

There is no established Tolerable Upper Intake Level for vitamin B12 since it has low potential for toxicity. Vitamin B12 is generally considered safe even at high doses, because our bodies excrete excess B12 through urine.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What type of B12 should vegans take?

    B12 is sold as either methylcobalamin, adenosylcobalamin, hydroxocobalamin, or cyanocobalamin. The most common form of B12 in supplements is cyanocobalamin, but the others are absorbed just as well. Geiger says the best type of vitamin B12 is the kind you will take. There are a variety of B12 supplements to choose from including sublingual, lozenges, liquid, and even powder forms. You should use a dose and form that suits your needs.

  • How much B12 should a vegan take daily?

    Geiger says vegans are recommended to take 2,000 mcg of B12 per week. Usually, this is split up into two doses of 1,000 mcg. Titcomb says that adults over 65 may need higher amounts at 1,000 mcg daily. If you are unsure about the correct dosage for your needs, we recommend speaking with a healthcare provider.

  • What are the symptoms of low B12?

    Symptoms of low B12 include fatigue or weakness, heart palpitations, pale skin, weight loss, loss of appetite, and infertility. You may also experience numbness or tingling in the hands and feet, problems with balance, depression, confusion, memory difficulties, dementia, and a sore mouth or tongue.
    The good news is that many of the effects of B12 deficiency can be reversed, especially in early stages of deficiency. A healthcare provider can help you get a blood test to check your levels, and if needed, can recommend a higher dose to help reverse deficiency.

  • What is the best form of B12 for vegans?

    The best way for vegans and vegetarians to get B12 is through supplements or food that is fortified with B12. Some foods, such as fortified cereals and nutritional yeast, have vitamin B12 added to them, but it is not enough to meet your daily needs. B12 supplements may help fill this gap and are available in sprays, pills, chewable tablets, liquid drops, and lozenges. Always speak to your healthcare provider prior to starting a new supplement.
