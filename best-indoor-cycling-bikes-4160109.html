  • How We Tested

    To find the best exercise bikes, we consulted cycling experts to learn more about the key features that set quality options apart. Then, we rode over 1,800 miles on 36 expert-approved bikes, completing a variety of workouts at least twice per week. When testing spin bikes, we evaluate each one based on the following key criteria.

    • Stability: We evaluate the stability of each indoor bike during both slow and intense rides. We also test the bike's ability to handle sudden changes in intensity or speed, both while riding and out of the saddle. We take note of any instability or wobbling, examining if the bike’s base is wide and heavy enough to support vigorous exercise. We also check for adjustable leveling feet to stabilize the bike on uneven floors.
    • Performance: We evaluate the smoothness and quietness of the bike's operation during use. We also note adjustable resistance, seats, and handlebars. Some bikes simulate a real-world outdoor cycling experience, so we pay attention to the ride's feel during uphill climbs and high-intensity sprints. We take note of any additional features, such as metric tracking, and how well they perform. We prioritize bikes that perform well and accommodate a range of fitness levels and workout intensities.
    • Adjustability: For bikes that offer adjustability, we evaluate these features and consider how easy they are to use. Is it simple to adjust the seat and handlebars to fit different body sizes? Can we adjust the resistance during our workout without stopping? We also identify any limitations to adjustability that could affect usability for some individuals.
    • Comfort: If you're uncomfortable on your bike, you won't want to use it. We consider how comfy the bike seat feels during short and long rides. We note whether an additional padded bike seat is necessary to ensure comfort while riding. Are the handlebars ergonomically designed to prevent strain on the wrists and arms? Does the bike accommodate standard cycling shoes or only specific types?
    • Display: For bikes with display screens, we evaluate their clarity and readability, as well as whether they accurately display relevant data, such as speed, distance, time, calories burned, and heart rate. We check for connectivity features, such as third-party app integration, and determine whether a membership is required to access the display's features. We also pay attention to the user-friendliness of the interface.
    • Value: To determine value, we don’t check each bike’s price until after evaluating it in all other key criteria. We consider whether we would pay this much for the bike or possibly even more. We prioritize bikes whose quality exceeds their value.

  • What to Look For

    • Resistance: When shopping for an exercise bike, consider the type of resistance system it uses—magnetic, air, or friction. Magnetic and air resistance are quieter, require less maintenance, and offer smoother transitions between resistance levels. On the other hand, friction resistance is often more budget-friendly and provides a broader range of resistance. Consider your workout needs and preferences when deciding which system suits you.
    • Pedals: Some exercise bikes feature toe-caged pedals that allow you to wear workout sneakers or cycling shoes, simply slipping your feet into the pedals. Others have clip-in pedals that require clipless cleats or cycling shoes. This type of pedal helps some riders to feel more secure and connected with their bike. But if your indoor cycling bike doesn't have the pedals you're looking for, it's an easy and, in most cases, inexpensive fix.
    • Workout Modes: Like treadmills, classic ellipticals, and other fitness machines, indoor bikes offer a range of preset workout modes. If you prefer a choose-your-own-adventure workout where you control your settings, these modes may not be a significant concern for you. But if you prefer a guided workout, pay attention to this feature.
    • Comfort: Comfort is critical, says certified cycling instructor Brooke Lutsch. If your bike is comfy, you’ll be more likely to use it regularly. To ensure comfort, look for a bike with a cushioned seat, padded handlebars, and wide pedals. Ensure these are adjustable, allowing you to customize your fit.
    • High-Tech Features: Many indoor bikes have high-tech features like speakers, fans, and pivoting monitors. Displays that feature your speed, or revolutions per minute (RPM), and power output (wattage) are always helpful for gauging your workout, says cycling expert Tammeca Rochester. These nice-to-haves may not be necessary, but they can make a workout more fun. Consider seeking features that will motivate you to spend more time on your bike.

Types of Exercise Bikes

  • Upright Bikes: An upright bike is designed with the seat positioned directly over the pedals and the handlebars closer to your body than those of other bikes. This allows you to stay in a more upright position, rather than leaning over the handlebars.
  • Recumbent Bikes: A recumbent bike features a chair-like seat with back support, and the pedals are positioned out in front of you rather than underneath. This style of bike reduces strain on the back and other joints while still providing a quality workout.
  • Indoor Cycling Bikes: Similar to an upright, an indoor cycling bike, also known as a spin bike, most closely mimics a road bike. It features a heavy flywheel that provides resistance for intense workouts, with handles positioned out in front, requiring you to lean over them. These bikes are what you typically see in cycling studios and classes.
  • Air Bikes: Air bikes are often used in CrossFit gyms and feature a large front "wheel" that is a fan. The fan provides the resistance, and these bikes usually have handles that move back and forth as you pedal.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 30 minutes of spinning a day enough?

A spin bike offers an excellent, low-impact workout that can be adjusted to suit varying intensities. Thirty minutes of exercise a day is sufficient for the average person to maintain their health and can serve as a good starting point.

What bike is just as good as a Peloton?

Peloton bikes are high-quality exercise bikes that offer the convenience of home workouts with a trainer-led experience, but the Peloton price point can be a deterrent for some. Many other brands, such as SoulCycle and Freebeat, offer similar features to a Peloton and are more budget-friendly.

What's the difference between a stationary bike and an exercise bike?

Often used interchangeably, stationary bikes and exercise bikes have some slight differences, but you can get a quality workout with either one. Stationary bikes encompass a variety of bike styles but generally refer to bikes designed for endurance workouts. Exercise bikes, or spin bikes, while still low-impact, have features that allow for much more intense workouts.

Is an exercise bike better than a treadmill?

An exercise bike offers a lower-impact yet still high-intensity workout compared to a standard treadmill. An exercise bike is also a better option if you have balance or mobility issues.
