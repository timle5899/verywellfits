How to Pick the Right Orthotics

  • Customization: The biggest factor differentiating custom orthotics from over-the-counter orthotics is the level of customization, says Schaeffer. While over-the-counter orthotics aren’t quite as customizable as prescription orthotics, some options are more customizable than others. First, look for adjustable arch support. Can you increase or decrease the amount of arch support your insoles offer? If not, can you choose the amount of arch support you want when you first purchase the insoles? Can you choose the precise size that suits your foot? What about the width and length? Is the insole trimmable, allowing you to adjust its size and shape to fit your shoes?
  • Credibility: Buying orthotic insoles for a specific health condition or pain point? Try to find options that are “clinically effective” at solving your problem. “That’s a label assurance that the product has been tested and shown to provide the benefits claimed on the label,” says Schaeffer. When shopping, you’ll encounter a lot of different claims, but don’t count on those claims until you know they’re backed by research.
  • Size: Orthotic insoles are available in various sizes, lengths, and widths. Some orthotics are available in specific shoe sizes, while others are offered in a range of sizes. Those that come in a size range are often trimmable, so you can cut them down to suit your feet. Second, ensure your orthotics are the correct length. Orthotics are typically designed to either fill the full length of your shoe or to fill three-quarters of your shoe. A podiatrist can help you understand which option is right for you. While most will come in one standard width, narrower and wider options are also available.
  • Arch Support: Orthotics are often available with different levels of arch support. Some orthotic insoles come in multiple arch support styles, allowing you to customize the level of arch support to suit your needs. Others are available with arch support pads, which you can use to adjust the arch support in your insoles after purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

What foot problems require orthotics?

Conditions that require orthotics include flat feet and high arches. Flat feet can lead to other conditions such as bunions, plantar fasciitis, and osteoarthritis. If you have a leg length discrepancy (one leg slightly longer than the other) or a lateral pelvic tilt (hip hike with hip higher on one side), you may require an orthotic on one side.

How long do orthotics last?

Orthotics can last three to five years, but you may need to replace them sooner, depending on changes to your physiology. These changes can include weight loss or gain, pregnancy, or other physical reasons. If you notice wear and tear, it is best to replace your orthotics.

How many hours a day should you wear orthotics?

If you have custom orthotics, it is best to wear them all day long to maintain their benefits. Orthotics correct issues that would otherwise go unaddressed when not being worn. If you are concerned about wearing them daily, speak to your podiatrist about options that may be best for you.
