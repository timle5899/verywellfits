What to Look for When Shopping for Effective Weighted Vests

  • Purpose: When shopping for a vest, consider how you plan to use it. If you want to run with a vest, you’ll need a lighter-weight one than if you’re going to walk or strength-train with it, says personal trainer Jack Craig, CPT.
  • Materials: Look for vests made of materials that are comfortable and moisture-wicking, such as Lycra, nylon, and neoprene. Durable fabrics with double-stitching also enhance a vest’s sturdiness.
  • Design: Closure straps—whether Velcro or buckles—are a key design feature, providing a more secure, customized fit while minimizing bounce and shifting during workouts. Storage pockets can also be beneficial for outdoor activities, such as running or hiking, offering space for essentials like water bottles and keys. Additionally, extra shoulder padding can help reduce friction and improve overall comfort, says personal trainer Jack Gardner, NASM-CPT.
  • Fixed vs. Adjustable: Vests with fixed weights are popular among fitness walkers and runners who want a small, consistent challenge. Be sure to choose a weighted vest that matches your fitness level and intended activities to avoid overloading. Adjustable weighted vests offer more flexibility, allowing you to change the resistance for different exercises and increase the weight as you become stronger, says Craig. Though typically pricier, they’re a worthwhile investment if you expect to use them long-term to improve your training and fitness.

How I Tested the Weighted Vests

To find the best weighted vests, I first spoke with fitness experts for their insights on what to look for in a quality vest. With this expert input in mind, I researched dozens of options from top, trusted brands. Then, I tested 15 weighted vests for a total of 90 hours, wearing them while walking, running, hiking, and strength training. When testing weighted vests, I rate them based on the following key attributes.

  • Fit: When I first try on the weighted vest, I evaluate how easily it can be tightened or loosened to achieve a snug, secure fit. Does it feel like it can accommodate different body shapes? Does the clothing I wear underneath it affect the fit?  As I move in the vest, I determine whether it stays in place comfortably.
  • Comfort: I put the vests through the rigors of various workouts, including bodyweight exercises, interval training, running, walking, and hiking, and note how comfortable they feel while I’m exercising. Does pressure from the vest cause any soreness or discomfort? Is there any digging, rubbing, or hot spots? I also note if the vest is breathable for at least thirty minutes of wear.
  • Weight Adjustability: For the adjustable weight vests, I test them with various loads to see if the level of performance or comfort changes depending on the weight used. I also note whether adding or removing the weight is easy and intuitive. Do the weight increments feel evenly distributed and manageable? For all the vests (whether fixed or adjustable), I determine if the minimum weight is safe and suitable for beginners.
  • Ease of Use: I evaluate whether I can easily put on and take off the vest without help. Are the adjustable points intuitive? I also note how long it takes to set up the vest and, if the weight is adjustable, I assess how simple it is to change the load.
  • Effectiveness: To evaluate the vest’s effectiveness, I pay attention to whether it noticeably increases the challenge of my workouts or activities. Does it maintain a balanced, even weight distribution during movements? I also check if I can keep good posture and achieve a full range of motion for both upper- and lower-body exercises.
  • Durability: At the end of each testing period, I inspect the vest to assess its durability. I examine the seams, closures, and adjustment mechanisms. Are there any signs of significant wear and tear? Is the vest affected by exposure to sweat?
  • Value: To assign a value score to each weighted vest, I consider its cost only after evaluating it against all the key attributes mentioned above. I consider whether the price is fair based on the vest's overall performance. I also consider whether I would pay full price or wait to purchase it on sale.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are weighted vests actually effective?

Weighted vests can be effective for boosting workout intensity, improving strength and balance, and increasing bone density. Their benefits depend on proper and consistent use; however, research suggests that they can be a useful tool for enhancing physical performance.

Is it okay to wear a weighted vest all day?

Wearing a weighted vest all day is not recommended due to the potential strain it may cause on your joints, muscles, neck, and spine. It's safer and more effective when used properly during specific workouts or for shorter durations. Beginners should be especially careful and make sure to ease into using a weighted vest, starting with 20- to 30-minute walking sessions once or twice a week, according to Montana.

Does walking with a weighted vest build muscle?

Weighted vest training can help build muscle and increase strength by adding resistance to walks, engaging your core, and intensifying your leg workout, says Craig. However, since it primarily targets the lower body, incorporating upper-body exercises is essential for a full-body routine.

Is a 20-pound weighted vest too much?

The weight of your vest should depend on your size, activity, and fitness level; lighter vests are ideal for cardio, while heavier ones are suited for strength training. Beginners using weighted vests for walking or cardio should start with 5 percent of their body weight, and more experienced users should aim for a maximum of 10 to 15 percent of their body weight, Wherry recommends.
