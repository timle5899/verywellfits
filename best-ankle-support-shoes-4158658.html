Ankle support shoes are essential for comfort and confidence if you have weak or injured ankles or experience ankle pain. As an exercise physiologist who occasionally suffers from ankle pain and mild overpronation, I understand the importance of selecting a pair of shoes with extra ankle support. Ankle instability can affect everything from exercise to daily routines, so I look for high-quality shoes that offer motion control, lateral support, arch support, and good traction.

  • How We Tested

    To find the best ankle support shoes, we consulted with four experts: two foot and ankle orthopedic surgeons, a physiatrist, and a podiatrist, for advice on what key features to look for. Then, we tested over 135 pairs of shoes in The Verywell Testing Lab and at home while walking, hiking, and working out on various even and uneven terrains. We evaluated each pair for its fit, comfort, responsiveness, stability, cushioning, traction, durability, and value to find the top options on the market. When testing ankle support shoes, we keep the following key questions in mind. 

    • Fit: When we put each shoe on for the first time, we note its initial fit: Is there a thumb’s width of space between our toe and the end of the shoe? Then, we start walking or running to see if there’s any rubbing, sliding, or shifting inside the shoe during movement. As we continue to wear-test each shoe, we pay attention to whether the fit and feel change over time. 
    • Comfort: If your shoe isn’t comfortable, you won’t wear it, so next up is assessing comfort. During each wear test, we pay attention to how the material feels: Are there any irritating seams? Do our feet feel overheated? Is there uncomfortable chafing or rubbing? We prioritize shoes that keep our feet cool, comfortable, and blister-free for hours on end. 
    • Responsiveness: Responsiveness refers to how effectively a shoe propels you to the next stride. To test for responsiveness, we spend weeks walking, running, and working out in each shoe to assess how springy or bouncy it feels when pushing off the ground. Shoes earn high scores for responsiveness if they prevent our legs and feet from feeling fatigued at the end of long walks, runs, or workouts. 
    • Stability: Stability is especially important when testing ankle support shoes. During our test walks and runs, as well as during daily wear, we note any knee or ankle instability. We also pay attention to shock absorption and impact protection. We prioritize shoes with additional motion control or stability features. 
    • Cushioning: We pay attention to how well the cushioning absorbs impact as our foot hits the ground. We also take note of any foot, knee, or leg instability, fatigue, or pain. Regardless of the level of cushioning—plush or firm—shoes earn high scores if they keep our feet comfortable and protected throughout testing. 
    • Traction: During testing, we use the shoes on various indoor and outdoor surfaces to evaluate the outsole’s grip. Do we experience any slipping or sliding while wearing the shoes on wet or slippery surfaces? Has the shoe’s traction deteriorated over time?
    • Durability: We pay attention to how the shoe holds up to regular use and wear, particularly in demanding activities or environments. Do we notice any signs of premature wear, such as fraying seams, peeling materials, or worn-out tread? Has the midsole worn down, or has the shoe's shape changed?
    • Value: After rigorously wear-testing each shoe, we consider its performance and price to determine whether it’s worth it. We prioritize shoes that we would consider purchasing at the listed price or even a higher price.

  • What to Look For

    • Motion Control: Shoes that offer motion control or extra stability features help decrease excessive movement in the foot and ankle, making them a good choice for those with weak ankles.
    • Support: Choose a shoe with a well-cushioned footbed for added support and improved shock absorption. And if you have a history of ankle injuries or ankle instability, look for a shoe that has sufficient arch support to accommodate your foot, while ensuring that the shape of the shoe and arch match your unique arch and alignment, says foot and ankle orthopedic surgeon Jonathan R. Kaplan, MD.
    • Sturdiness: Look for shoes with durable, sturdy soles and firm heel counters. The heel counter should be rigid to hold your heel in place, helping to stabilize your ankle, says podiatrist Nelya Lobkova, DPM.
    • Drop: A shoe’s heel-to-toe drop measures the difference between the height of the shoe at the heel and at the forefoot. Its drop affects your cadence, foot strike, and what part of your body takes on the load or impact of each step. In running or walking shoes, the drop typically ranges from 0 to 12 millimeters. A high heel-to-toe drop encourages a heel-strike gait, which can lead to more impact forces on the heel and ankle, thereby increasing the risk of injury to those areas. On the other hand, shoes with lower heel-to-toe drops promote a more natural or forefoot strike and place less pressure on your knees, ankles, and hips, which can help prevent injuries in those areas.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is ankle support important?

Ankle support helps protect you from painful foot injuries that often require months of recovery, physical therapy, and—in some cases—even surgery. Wearing a supportive shoe while walking or running can not only reduce the likelihood of injury should a misstep occur, but also aid in the mechanics of these activities, says Kaplan.

What is the best way to tie shoes for ankle support?

Some sneakers have an extra eyelet at the top that you can use for additional ankle support. If not, lace your shoes all the way up through the top eyelets and tie a tight double-knot to secure the laces, ensuring a snug fit around the ankle.

What shoes should you wear if you have weak ankles?

Look for a good pair of ankle support shoes with a rigid heel counter that wraps around the heel and ankle for stability. The shoe should fit snugly to provide support, and you should be able to tie the laces tightly to aid the snug fit.
